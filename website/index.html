Key Findings
What We Discovered

Our evaluation across 27 vision-language models reveals systematic patterns in how VLMs handle Pok√©mon recognition.

Finding 01

Proprietary Models Dominate

The top 7 models are all proprietary ‚Äî GPT-4.1 leads Master Ball at 79%. The best open-weight model (Qwen3-VL 4B) manages only 13.4%. But relaxing to Great Ball, Gemini 2.5 Pro reaches 91.4%, showing most errors are form/variant confusion rather than total misidentification.

Finding 02

Silhouettes Humble Everyone

Stripping color devastates performance. GPT-4.1 drops from 79% to 49%, and Gemini 2.5 Pro plummets from 78% to 23%. Only Claude 4.5 Sonnet actually leads on silhouettes at 53%, flipping the HQ leaderboard on its head.

Finding 03

Claude's Shadow Ball Surprise

Claude 4.5 Sonnet ranks 4th on HQ images (65%) but takes the crown on silhouettes (53%) ‚Äî the only model where shadow performance exceeds half. Claude Haiku 4.5 shows near-identical scores on both modes (~27%), suggesting a uniquely robust shape understanding.

Finding 04

Gen 8 Is the Frontier ‚Äî Gen 2 Is Solved

Proprietary models average 73.4% on Gen 2 but only 39.2% on Gen 8 ‚Äî a 34-point gap. Gen 6 is a surprising weak spot at 48.4%, likely due to many visually similar small Pok√©mon. Open-weight models collapse after Gen 1, falling to under 2% from Gen 3 onward.

Finding 05

Open-Weight Gen 1 Hallucination

When open-weight models guess wrong, 57% of guesses on HQ and 79% on silhouettes are Gen 1 Pok√©mon ‚Äî despite Gen 1 being only 19.6% of the dataset. These models have heavily memorized iconic Pok√©mon like Pikachu and Charizard and default to them when uncertain. Proprietary models show only mild bias (~24%).

Finding 06

Form-Blind Models

Most models are nearly blind to variant forms. Claude 4.5 Sonnet hits 82.8% on base forms but 2.1% on Megas and 0% on Gigantamax. Only GPT-4.1 handles Megas (87.5%) and regional forms (63.6%). 162 Pok√©mon get zero correct across all 27 models ‚Äî nearly all are variant forms like Pikachu caps and Deoxys formes.

Finding 07

Fire Types Are Easiest, Electric Hardest

Among proprietary models, Fire-type Pok√©mon are identified most accurately at 65.7%, while Electric types trail at 51.0% ‚Äî a 14.7-point spread. Dragon (51.5%) and Bug (53.0%) types also prove difficult, possibly because these categories contain many visually similar species. The type ranking stays consistent between proprietary and open-weight models.

Finding 08

Color-Dependent Recognition

Some Pok√©mon are 100% on HQ but 0% as silhouettes ‚Äî Magmortar's fiery details vanish into a generic blob. Slugma, Galvantula, and Alcremie all drop 85+ points. These Pok√©mon are recognized by color and texture, not shape ‚Äî exposing a fundamental reliance on surface-level features.

Finding 09

Shape-Advantaged Pok√©mon

A few Pok√©mon are better recognized as silhouettes than as full artwork. Absol jumps from 85.7% to 100% ‚Äî its distinctive scythe-horn outline is unmistakable. Scizor and Mega Charizard X also gain +14 points, suggesting their silhouettes are more iconic than their colored forms.

Getting Started
Evaluate Your Model

WTP-Bench is lightweight and straightforward. Clone the repo, plug in your model, and run evaluation in minutes. 

# Clone the repository
git clone https://github.com/eternal-f1ame/WTP-Bench.git
cd WTP-Bench && pip install -r requirements.txt

# Run inference with your model
python src/scripts/vlm_infer_template.py \
  --input-csv data/metadata.csv \
  --output-csv predictions/my_model_hq.csv \
  --prompt "Who's that Pokemon?" \
  --model-id your-model-id \
  --base-dir data

# Evaluate predictions
PYTHONPATH=src python src/scripts/eval_predictions.py \
  --pred-csv predictions/my_model_hq.csv
